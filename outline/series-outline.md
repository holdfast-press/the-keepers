# THE KEEPERS
## Series Outline — v0.2

*A roadmap, not a contract. Book One is locked. Books Two through Six are sketched with conviction but open to revision as we write. The shape is what matters here.*

---

## Series Concept

A modern-world series in which a hidden council of memory-bearers — souls displaced from older bindings into the present day — slowly find each other across the world's IT conference circuit and the everyday accidents of modern life, and realize that the bindings their long-dead bloodlines once held are failing one by one. Each book travels to a different culture, a different mythology, a different keeper-line, a different sleeping thing. The duty is the same duty. The cast — anchored by two — rotates.

**Tone, sustained across the series:** Galaxy Quest's heart and humor. LOTR's weight and worldbuilding. The lived texture of the Microsoft conference circuit as the cover under which everything else happens. Adult fiction (R-rated equivalent), with real loss and real intimacy and real grief, but never grimdark. The friendships are the soul of every book.

**Planned length:** Six books, with horizon room for more if the world keeps producing stories. *Each book must stand on its own as a satisfying read.* No cliffhangers between books. Book-level threats resolve; book-level character arcs land. What carries between books is the world, the anchor characters, and a small number of *seeds* — quiet things that don't haunt a single-book reader but reward the long-haul reader who keeps going.

---

## Series Theme — *Locked*

> **"What we choose to carry, carries us."**

The bindings. The friendships. The dead. The duty. None of them are burdens you escape. They are things you accept, and in accepting them, they become how you stay alive.

- Hal carries Beth and Maya, and they carry him.
- The council carries Callum, and Callum carries them.
- The keepers carry their bloodlines' bindings, and the bindings carry the world.
- Marcus carries his choice to be part of this even though he didn't have to, and his choice carries the keepers across the threshold of Book Four.

The central antagonist of the series (see below) is the one who tried to make the keepers stop carrying. He wants them to drop what they hold so he can take what is underneath. The keepers' answer across six books is: *we hold harder. We hold smarter. We carry each other.*

Every plot decision, every character beat, every loss in the series serves this theme. If something doesn't, we cut it.

## The Working's Reach

The series follows the structural model laid out in detail in `the_keepers_book_one_outline.md`: rotating cast, anchor characters, memory as load-bearing device, loss with weight. The structural reference points are Tolkien's *Hobbit → LOTR* and CS Lewis's *Chronicles of Narnia.* Both authors let the framework stay constant while the protagonists rotated, both used memory and flashback as story-shape rather than flavor, and both allowed beloved characters to genuinely leave — and then carried them through every book that followed.

**What the working did:** It took the keepers from Eikvik. Where it took them — what world they woke in, what era, what distance was crossed — is the question the series answers across Books 1-3. The characters don't know. The reader doesn't know yet. What is known: the keepers exist, the bindings are failing, and the duty holds no matter what shore they woke on.

For our purposes:
- **The framework is constant.** The keepers, the bindings, the Eikvik backstory, the Microsoft conference circuit as the modern crew's connective tissue, the slow accretion of the global council. *What world that circuit exists on is load-bearing mystery — do not resolve it in Book 1.*
- **The cast rotates.** Each book centers a different keeper or pair of keepers and a different bloodline. The crew from Book One is present across the series, but they don't all show up in every book, and when they do, sometimes they lead and sometimes they support.
- **The anchors are constant.** Hal and Erin appear in every book.
- **Memory is the engine.** Past lives. Old verses. Dreams. Things characters did long ago that catch up to them later. A line in Book 1 returns in Book 4 with new meaning.
- **The dead stay dead — and stay present.** Callum is gone after Book 1. He is carried through every book that follows.

## Anchor Characters — *In Every Book*

**Hal Bergstrom / Halvard.** The Keeper of Eikvik's binding. By the end of Book 1 he has accepted the vow. Across the series he learns what it actually means to hold one. He ages with the books. He gets tired. He gets wiser. He becomes the mentor to keepers younger than himself. The spine of the series is the slow rebuilding of a man who lost everything four years before the series began, and who has to figure out — across six books — what it means to be alive again, to be loved again, to have a family again.

**Erin Sigrid Halvorsen / Sigrún.** The Keeper of voice and memory. By the end of Book 1 she is the only living person who can speak the old workings aloud. Across the series she becomes the council's lore-keeper — the one who learns each new bloodline's verses, the one who teaches each new keeper their own forgotten language. Her arc with Hal does not rush. They are not married at the end of Book 1. They are partners. The series gives them years.

These two are the Gandalf and the Aslan of this series, in the sense that they are *felt* in every book even when they aren't on every page.

## Returning Cast — *The Rotating Crew*

These are the characters who appear across multiple books, with their primary roles and approximate book-leads:

**Jan Bjornstad / Jorund.** The builder. The steady one. The boat he built in his garage gets used and used again — in Book 1 as part of the working, in later books as a literal vessel the council uses on water-bound errands. *Possibly leads or co-leads Book 4. His son Magnus begins to manifest gifts in Book 3 or 4 — the first indication that the bloodlines continue to produce keepers.*

**Stefan Vogel / Asbjorn.** The smith, the convert. Carries the heaviest doubt-turned-faith. *Leads Book 4 — the binding under industrial Hamburg.* Crisis of faith again somewhere mid-series, and the others have to help him through it the way he once needed help.

**Annika de Vries** (villager, bondi-woman). The lore-keeper before Erin had her own language back. *Her Amsterdam home becomes the council's de facto European hub from Book 2 onward.* Possibly leads Book 5.

**Tony Reyes** (villager, trader of Vikvágr). The connector. *Leads Book 5 — when the council travels to the Andes, his Spanish bridges them to the local keeper-line.* His foster children with Marco become recurring presences across the series; one of them eventually shows signs of being a keeper themselves, opening a thread we'll follow.

**Mel Carter** (non-villager, Sydney). Loyalty incarnate. Stays across the series. Becomes one of the council's most trusted operational minds — the one who figures out logistics in places the keepers have never been. Comedy duo with Callum in Book 1; comedy widow across the rest, which is its own thing.

**Hamish Reid** (non-villager, Auckland). The quiet one who picks up the bar tab. Stays. His role grows slowly. By Book 4 or 5 he is the one the others trust to make decisions under fire — partly because he has nothing to prove.

**Mihai Stancu** (non-villager, Toronto via Bucharest). Five languages. The skill that looks like comic relief in Book 1 becomes load-bearing in Book 3 — Mihai leads the Romania book. His Bucharest childhood, his Orthodox grandmother who insisted on certain rituals, his five languages — these turn out to be a *different* kind of inheritance.

**Sarah Wexler** (non-villager, Bay Area). The youngest. The outsider who keeps choosing this family. Her arc is the most quietly powerful non-villager arc in the series — *she is the one who makes the council ask the real question across all six books: who deserves to be a keeper? The ones born to it, or the ones who chose it?* In Book 4, she is the first to learn Marcus's fate, and the way Sarah carries that across Books 5 and 6 is what gives her question its answer.

## Marcus Chen — *The Long Arc to Book Four*

Marcus's death is a planned beat across the series. Because he isn't a villager and isn't on the main plot's drum-beat, we have to track him visibly book-by-book so the reader is fully invested in him by the time he dies. The arc:

- **Book One** — Already here. Road trip to the Hebrides. Annika tries to explain what is happening to him in the van outside Glasgow. Perimeter and support role at Callanish — he is *outside* the circle when the working completes, watching. Callum's funeral in Edinburgh. He is established as the *normal-life thread* the council still has.
- **Book Two (Japan)** — Doesn't physically go. Chicago DevOps guy with a regular life and a job and a family. Present on the group chat. **One scene where he flies down to Austin between Hal's Japan trips just to make sure Hal is eating.** That scene is the seed for everything to come. Marcus is the friend who shows up when no one asked.
- **Book Three (Romania)** — Goes. Mihai brings the whole crew to his country. Marcus sees keepers do something openly for the first time — not in shadow, not after the fact, but in the open. It scares him. He almost quits. He doesn't. The chapter where he sits in a Bucharest hotel room at 2 a.m. on the phone with his mother in Chicago, not telling her anything but needing to hear her voice, is a key chapter for him.
- **Book Four (Hamburg)** — Asks to come. **Stefan tries to talk him out of it.** Comes anyway. By midpoint of the book the crew understands they need an anchor for the climactic working — someone outside the circle who can hold a position the keepers cannot. *If a keeper does it, they are trapped in the binding — locked there forever, a kind of living death. If a non-keeper does it, they don't have the soul-stuff to survive contact with the working. They die.* Marcus volunteers because he is the only one who *can.* He is the only one in the room without a bloodline.

His dying words are small. Not a speech. Something like: ***"Tell my mom I wasn't scared."***

The thematic point: the keepers were *born* to this. Marcus *chose* it. And the cost only a non-keeper could pay is the cost he chooses to pay. That is the answer to Sarah Wexler's question — *who deserves to be a keeper?* — and it is the answer that breaks everyone. Sarah finds out first, in her POV chapter.

After Book Four, Marcus is gone but carried. The council lifts a glass for him in every subsequent book, alongside Callum.

## Callum — *The Lost One Carried Forward*

Callum dies in Book 1, Chapter 17. He does not return as a ghost, a vision, or a fakeout. He stays dead.

He is also present in every book that follows. Mechanisms:

- **His verses.** Erin transcribed his death-chant. It becomes one of the foundational workings the council uses. Every time it is spoken, Callum is invoked by definition.
- **His grandmother on Lewis.** She knew. She remembered enough of the old ways to recognize her grandson before he recognized himself. She is a recurring figure on the Outer Hebrides through the early-middle books, until she dies offscreen between books — a death the crew mourns at the croft.
- **His son in the Hebrides.** Past-side, Callum had Una and an infant son in the Hebrides who survived the working. *Modern Scotland still carries that bloodline.* One of his modern descendants becomes a recurring presence from Book 3 onward — a quiet, unassuming Scotsman who, at a certain conference in Edinburgh, looks across a hotel bar at the crew and they all see Callum in him for a moment that nobody can talk about.
- **The lifted glass.** Every book has at least one scene where the crew is at a hotel bar together and someone, without preamble, lifts a glass and says his name. Everyone goes quiet for a second. Then someone tells the consulting-disaster story for the fortieth time and they laugh through tears. **After Book Four, the lifted glass is for both Callum and Marcus.**

## Memory Across the Series — *How Past Lives Work*

After the working at Callanish completes, **memories don't go away — but they aren't constantly present either.** Like learning a second language as an adult. You have it. You can access it when you need it. But you don't think in it.

- Hal isn't walking around Austin in Halvard-mode. He is Hal. But when the council needs to know how the working felt, or what Eirik Greybeard said at the strand, or what runes were carved on which stones — he can reach for it.
- Erin's *völva* training works the same way. She has it, she can call on it, but she is still Erin Halvorsen with her cryptography job in Zurich.
- Stefan can smith a rune-stone with his hands. But he is still Stefan from Hamburg with his industrial loft and his daughters from his first marriage.

This gives us the right shape for each book going forward. Each new bloodline's keepers haven't had their working remade yet. They start their book in the *memories surfacing* state — the way Hal and the crew were in Part Two of Book One. By the end of their book, after their binding is remade, they have their past-life memories integrated the same way the Eikvik keepers do.

**Same dramatic shape every time, but with different cultures, different threats, and different keepers — so it doesn't feel formulaic.** And it lets the Eikvik characters do the *teaching* role in later books — Hal and Erin have already walked this path, so they can recognize where the new keepers are on it.

## Series Arc — The Cosmology Reveal

The series carries a central mystery from the first page: what the seer's working actually did, where it took the keepers, and what world they woke in. **This mystery is load-bearing. Do not resolve it in Book 1.**

**Reveal cadence:**
- **Book 1:** The mechanism is unknown. Keepers discover they have past lives from a 9th-century Norse village. They accept the vow. The reader does not learn what the working was or where it sent them — only that something vast took them, and that Brand came through too.
- **Book 2 (Japan):** The world feels almost-right in ways the keepers can't name. The Kyoto keeper and her bloodline feel *different* from the Eikvik keepers in a way that nags. Seeds planted, not resolved.
- **Book 3 (Romania):** The partial reveal. Mid-book: the nature of the working begins to come clear. By the end of Book 3 the keepers understand the cosmology. The reader understands it with them.
- **Books 4-6:** The keepers inhabit the wider canvas the reveal opens. Cross-realm travel becomes possible, then necessary, then the spine of the series.

**Series geography (escalating outward):**
- **Book 1** — Modern Earth (Hill Country, Lewis). As written. The world seems like ours.
- **Book 2** — Modern Earth. Strangeness accumulates quietly.
- **Book 3** — Crossings begin. A character steps through and comes back changed.
- **Book 4** — An act takes place in another realm.
- **Book 5** — Primarily in a mythic realm. The Andes of *this* world sit on something that was never only this world.
- **Book 6** — Crossing-war. The full canvas opens.

**The structural principle:** What is constant is the *keepers, the bindings, the duty, and the friendships* — not the cosmological setting. The series earns its multi-realm canvas by keeping Book 1 fully grounded and letting the mystery deepen slowly. The prologue and Chapter 1 already do this correctly. Protect them.

## Linking the Books — *How One Threads Into the Next*

**Book One closes its threat completely.** The Hungering Earth is re-bound. Brand is unmade. Hal and the crew have done their work.

But **Brand wasn't only suppressing Hal's bloodline.** He was an instrument of the larger pattern (see Series-Level Antagonist below), and his death at Callanish removed a piece of the suppression worldwide. Keepers in other bloodlines, in other countries, who have been dreaming partial dreams their whole lives, finally wake up enough to act.

So the final image of Book One is a deliberate amplification. At Hal's kitchen table in Austin: **not one letter — three.** From three different countries. From three different women and men who have been having dreams of Hal's face for decades and finally feel called to write. **The Kyoto letter is the first one Hal opens, because the handwriting on the envelope catches him.** That woman becomes the new-keeper protagonist of Book Two.

The other two letters wait. They are seeds — the kind that don't haunt a single-book reader but reward the long-haul reader. One of them is from Romania. One is from elsewhere. (We will plant them visibly without naming them.)

The same pattern repeats book-to-book. **Each book closes the immediate threat fully — but each ending plants one to three letters / signals / arrivals for what comes next.** A reader can stop at the end of any book and have a complete experience. A reader who continues finds the next bloodline already calling.

## Tentative Book-by-Book Roadmap

*Settings, mythologies, and titles below are proposals to give the series shape. They are not locked. As we draft each book, the previous book's events will reshape the next.*

### Book One — *What the Stones Remember* (LOCKED)

**Setting:** Trøndelag (past) / Microsoft conference circuit, Reykjavik, Outer Hebrides (modern).
**Mythology:** Norse + pre-Norse.
**Entity:** The Hungering Earth.
**Antagonist:** Brand Wolfsbane / "Wes."
**New keeper(s) introduced:** Hal, Erin, Jan, Stefan, Annika, Tony.
**Lost:** Callum.
**Closes:** The Hungering Earth re-bound. Brand unmade.
**Plants:** Three letters on Hal's kitchen table. The seed line from Brand's final moments (see Must-Do list).

### Book Two — *(working title: The Mountain That Should Not Wake)*

**Setting:** Modern Japan + the old shrines and mountain-bindings of Tōhoku and Honshū.
**Mythology:** Shinto + pre-Shinto + the older folkloric layer beneath Shinto.
**Entity:** A sleeping thing bound beneath one of the sacred mountains.
**Antagonist:** A Japanese counterpart to Brand. Operative of the same central figure who ran Brand. *This is when the reader and the council first realize the pattern.*
**New keeper(s) introduced:** **The Kyoto woman** whose letter ends Book 1. Her name and bloodline are TBD. Her own version of the crew also begins to surface.
**Returning cast:** Hal and Erin (anchors), with Mihai prominent, Annika as lore-keeper bridging, Stefan in a supporting role, the rest by phone/group chat from elsewhere.
**Sub-thread:** Hal navigates being a *visitor* in someone else's culture and someone else's working. Book One was *his* country, his bones. Book Two is not. This is humbling for him, and it should be.
**Marcus:** Doesn't go. Scene: flies to Austin between Hal's Japan trips just to make sure Hal is eating.
**Plants:** Two more letters at the close.

### Book Three — *(working title: The Old Roads)*

**Setting:** Romania / Carpathians / the Slavic-Romanian folkloric north of the country.
**Mythology:** Slavic + Romanian + older Dacian layers. Rusalka. Strigoi. The land that remembers what's underneath.
**Lead:** **Mihai** is the centerpiece. The book where the comic-relief character becomes the man whose work matters most. Mihai is not a villager — he has not been a soul-keeper. But his five languages, his Bucharest childhood, his Orthodox grandmother who insisted on certain rituals — those turn out to be a *different* kind of inheritance.
**Returning cast:** Hal, Erin, Jan (Magnus, Jan's son, shows the first sign of his own gift in this book; Jan is torn), Stefan, Mel.
**Marcus:** Comes. Sees keepers do something openly for the first time. Almost quits. Doesn't.
**Sub-thread:** *Sarah Wexler's question — who deserves to be a keeper? — gets a real first answer here.* Mihai chose this family before he knew there was anything to choose.

### Book Four — *(working title: Below the Foundry)*

**Setting:** Germany / industrial Hamburg / the deep history beneath modern industry. The binding here is younger than Eikvik's — bound by Stefan's ancestors at the working of a forge during the early medieval period.
**Lead:** **Stefan.** The book where the smith becomes the keeper. His grandfather's deathbed words from Book 1 finally make full sense. Stefan's crisis of faith in this book is the central conflict — he believes, then doesn't, then has to learn that doubt is not the opposite of being a keeper.
**Returning cast:** Hal, Erin, Annika (close to home), Jan, Tony.
**Marcus:** **Marcus dies.** He volunteers to anchor the climactic working from outside the circle — the only one in the room without a bloodline, and therefore the only one who *can.* Dying words: *"Tell my mom I wasn't scared."* Sarah Wexler finds out first.
**Hal and Erin:** Married, quietly, at Callanish, with the crew. No press release. No ceremony Sarah Wexler's mother would recognize.

### Book Five — *(working title: The Stone Throat of the Andes)*

**Setting:** Peru / Bolivia / the Andean highlands. Cusco. Lake Titicaca. The ruins above the cloud-line that were old when the Inca arrived.
**Mythology:** Aymara, Quechua, pre-Inca. The bindings here are *high* — bound on mountaintops, not under them. The hungering thing in this book pulls *upward*, which is a different kind of dread.
**Lead:** **Tony** bridges. His Spanish opens the door; his nature as a connector opens the people. *One of Tony and Marco's foster children, now grown, comes along.*
**Returning cast:** Hal, Erin, Mel, Hamish (the southern-hemisphere half of the crew gets to lead the logistics).
**Sub-thread — The Beth and Maya Vision:** At altitude, in a place-between-worlds at the top of a sacred Quechua peak, **Hal sees Beth and Maya.** Mechanics matter here:
- Hal goes up alone. Keepers always go up alone for these things.
- Before he goes, **Erin tells him: *"Look for Beth."*** Erin already knows. Erin has been waiting for Hal to be able to do this.
- **Beth's first words are about Erin.** *"She loves you well. I see her. Tell her thank you."*
- **Maya — being seven, and being Maya — asks about something small only a daughter would ask.** Maybe about Hal's coffee. Maybe about whether Erin still keeps the porch light on. Something that wrecks the reader because it's so specific.
- Beth and Maya go. Hal comes down. Erin is waiting at the bottom. Neither speaks for a long time.

The point: Beth blesses Erin. Erin already knew Beth needed to. Hal gets to grieve forward instead of backward for the rest of his life.

### Book Six — *(working title: The Last Council)*

**Setting:** Egypt / Sinai / the convergence. The council gathers from every previous book — the Norse keepers, the Kyoto keeper, the Romanian keeper, the smith, the Andean keepers, the Hebridean descendants of Callum, all of them.
**Mythology:** Coptic + ancient Egyptian + older. The pattern beneath the pattern.
**Antagonist:** **The central figure is named and faced.** The being who warned Brand. The one who has been exploiting the cosmic shift across centuries.
**Lead:** A collective lead, in the Narnia *Last Battle* model. Hal and Erin are at the center but they are not alone. Every previous protagonist returns. Every loss returns to be carried.
**Closes:** The central figure is unmade. The cosmic shift is *not* — that, the keepers have to learn to live with. The cost is real. **Some of the anchors do not walk away.** *(Hal? Erin? Open question — to be earned by what the previous books have done.)*

## Hal & Erin Across the Series

The relationship is the slow burn that has to feel real across years, not chapters.

- **Book 1:** They find each other. They have a single charged hotel-bar conversation in Berlin. They sit on Maya's bedroom floor at 3 a.m. and hold each other in a doorway. They choose each other at the stones at Callanish.
- **Book 2:** They are dating. The complications are real — they live on different continents, on opposite ends of an industry that constantly puts them in airports. They figure it out one trip at a time. There is real intimacy in this book, off-screen but present.
- **Book 3:** They are living together — slowly, awkwardly, in Hal's Austin house, after Maya's room becomes a guest room. This is the book where Hal finally repaints Maya's room, with Erin holding the roller beside him.
- **Book 4:** They are married. Quietly. At Callanish, with the crew.
- **Book 5:** They are talking about children. The question is whether Hal can. Whether either of them can. **The Beth-and-Maya vision happens in this book, and it changes the shape of that conversation — but doesn't necessarily answer it.**
- **Book 6:** Whatever the final cost of the series is, it falls on them together.

## Series-Level Antagonist — *Locked: The Hybrid*

**The central figure (a coordinating intelligence) has been exploiting an underlying cosmic shift (an age ending) across centuries.**

There IS a coordinating person — call him for now **the Old One** *(true name TBD; we will likely never give him a real-world name, only a title that the keepers learn to fear).* He is something like Brand was, but older. He was Brand's teacher or peer or patron — someone Brand looked up to. He has been working across centuries to unmake bindings worldwide, picking off one bloodline at a time, exploiting the larger weakening of the world to make his work easier and harder to detect.

He CAN be faced. He CAN be unmade. That happens in Book Six.

But the cosmic shift cannot. The bindings are failing globally because **the world itself is changing — an age ending.** Even after the Old One is gone, the keepers have to learn that part of their duty is *holding what can be held while the world becomes a different world.* Some things they save. Some things they have to let go with grace.

This gives the series a real fight to win in Book Six **and** a deeper truth that the keepers must accept anyway.

### Brand's Seed Line — *Must Do*

In Book One Chapter 23, in Brand's final moments at Callanish, he says something like:

> ***"He warned me you'd come at it like this."***

That single sentence is the planted seed for the entire series. Readers won't know who *he* is on first read. Long-haul readers will know exactly who it was by Book Six. The line goes in. Locked.

8. **The keepers' memory model** — past lives accessible like a second language, never overriding modern life.

## Open Questions

These remain genuinely unanswered and are open for revision as we draft.

- **Does Hal survive the series? Does Erin?** Book Six will earn this. Whatever falls on them, falls on them together.
- **The final image of the series.** What does the last beat of Book Six look like? Equivalent to the letter at the end of Book One — but for a series, not a book.
- **Callum's modern Hebridean descendant.** Name, age, when he first appears. Likely Book 3, but his exact role across Books 3-6 is open.
- **The Old One's name and shape.** We don't need to know this until Book Five at the latest. We need to *plant* him from Book One forward (via Brand's seed line and the second/third letters) without naming him.
- **The two non-Kyoto letters at the end of Book One.** What countries, what bloodlines? Likely Romania (which becomes Book 3) and one other. The "one other" sets up Book 4 or 5.
