## The Imprint & The Byline — *Foundational Names*

These are the names everything else hangs from. Locking them first means every downstream decision (domain, org, social handles, byline on the cover, copyright page) follows automatically and consistently.

### The Byline

**Public-facing author name (everywhere):** **R.J. Thornton**

**Legal / title-page / copyright name:** Rurik Jesper Thornton — a strict anagram of the author's real name (every letter accounted for, none added). Used on the title page, copyright registration, and any place a legal author name is required. Pronounced *ROO-rik YES-per THORN-ton.*

**Real name:** Stays private. Not used on any public-facing surface.

The shorthand byline **R.J. Thornton** is the only name the public ever sees. On the cover spine, on the back of the book, on social handles, on the website, in interviews, in the copyright notice if you choose, in everything. The full name *Rurik Jesper Thornton* exists for the same reason J.K. Rowling has a "Joanne Rowling" buried in legal paperwork: it's the name on file, not the name on the brand.

**Why this works for the work:**
- *Rurik* was the legendary Varangian (Viking-era Norseman) who founded the Rurikid dynasty. The first name is unmistakably Norse, quietly signaling to a reader of *The Keepers* that the author knows the register.
- *Jesper* is the Scandinavian form of Jasper, ultimately from a Persian word for "treasure-bearer." A keeper's name, if you stop and listen to it.
- *Thornton* grounds the Norse first/middle names in an Anglo literary surname. The pen name itself does in miniature what the series does in macro: Norse heritage meeting modern Anglo soil.
- *R.J. Thornton* shortens to a byline that belongs on a thriller-rack shelf alongside J.K. Rowling, J.R.R. Tolkien, P.D. James.

**Cover and spine treatment:** **R.J. THORNTON** set in Cinzel small caps. Quiet authority. Always positioned beneath the book title, never above.

### The Imprint

**Imprint name:** **Holdfast Press**

**Tagline:** *Books that hold.*

**Why Holdfast:** *Holdfast* is an Old English / Old Norse word meaning a stronghold, a thing that endures — also the verb itself, *to hold fast.* It is, in a single word, what the series is about. *"What we choose to carry, carries us"* and *"the keepers hold the line"* are the same idea said two ways. Holdfast names the same idea a third way.

The word has the right gravity for a Lawhead/Lewis/Tolkien-adjacent shelf — old-English-mythic without being costume-y — and it scales beyond *The Keepers* to anything else published under the imprint later. No existing "Holdfast Press" turns up in the major fantasy-publisher directories, so the namespace appears available to claim.

**Imprint scope:** Holdfast Press publishes R.J. Thornton's literary fantasy work. *The Keepers* is the first series. Future series live here as well. Other properties under other registers (e.g. Gunner the Lab, which sits in a different audience entirely) do NOT live under Holdfast — they keep their separate identities. The imprint is for the literary-fantasy bookshelf, not for everything you'll ever write.

**Imprint mark:** The broken-binding glyph (specified in the Identity Decisions section below) doubles as the Holdfast Press mark when set at small scale at the base of the spine or on the copyright page. The mark belongs to the imprint first, the series second; this allows future Holdfast books to share visual DNA without each needing its own identity from scratch.

**Imprint colophon copy** *(for the copyright page of every Holdfast book):*

> *Holdfast Press*
> *Books that hold.*
> *holdfastpress.com*

**Runner-up imprint name** *(if Holdfast is unavailable or doesn't land on reflection):* **Bonefire Press.** Etymologically the original Anglo-Saxon word for funerary fires lit with bones — the prologue opens on a bonfire on the strand. Darker and more atmospheric than Holdfast, but more genre-specific; pigeonholes the imprint more narrowly. Take Holdfast for longevity; take Bonefire for pure atmosphere.

## Brand Position — *The Two Registers*

The Keepers brand has one job: hold two visual registers in tension so the reader feels the book before they read a word of it.

**Register One — the rune-haunt.** Fog over a fjord. Wet wool. Old copper. A great stone door shutting somewhere above you. Hammered iron. Lichen. Paper that looks like it was buried for a while. The patient attention turning underneath everything.

**Register Two — the modern texture.** A Marriott hotel bar at midnight. Conference lanyards like leashes. A Lagunitas IPA. Fluorescent meeting rooms at Moscone. The bay laid out in pieces behind the glass on the 39th floor. A phone face-down on a four-top.

The Prologue ends on *"a hotel alarm clock, beeping in the dark."* That single beat is the brand. A site that's all rune-stone-and-fog reads as generic dark fantasy. A site that's all hotel-bar-and-IT-jokes reads as quirky-but-light. The interesting thing — the thing that makes a stranger commit to a 24-chapter novel about Viking-era IT consultants — is **both at once.** The rune carved into the side of the elevator panel. The standing stone photographed in cold November daylight. The hotel alarm clock with runic numerals.

**Series theme as brand spine:** *"What we choose to carry, carries us."*

Every visual decision in this doc is in service of those two registers and that one line.

The palette is deliberately desaturated. Ember is the only chromatic color and shows up in maybe one in twenty assets — it earns its weight by being rare.

### Typography

| Role | Font | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Display (titles, covers, banners) | **Cormorant Garamond SemiBold** | Free via Google Fonts. Classic serif with a hint of stone-carved character. The italic is gorgeous for taglines. |
| Headers (site, marketing) | **Cinzel** | Free. Roman-capital feel that reads slightly runic without being costume-y. |
| Body (site, ebook) | **Lora** | Free. Highly readable serif, holds long-form text well. |
| Accent (modern-register moments) | **IBM Plex Mono** | Free. The "conference-badge" voice. Use for footers, metadata, in-world UI moments. |

All four are free, web-safe, ebook-safe. If you ever want to upgrade to paid type, the natural step up is **Hagia Sofia Pro** (display) and **Tiempos Text** (body), but the free stack is genuinely good.

### The Mark — *The Broken Binding*

The series is literally about bindings that are failing. The mark should *be* that.

**Concept:** A Norse interlace knot drawn as a single continuous line forming a circular ring — except at one point, the line breaks. A single short gap. The ring almost holds. It doesn't quite.

This is your sigil. It scales. It works as a favicon at 16×16 (just a ring with a tick out of it). It works at 4K as a hero element. It tells the whole story of the series in one glyph. As the series progresses, the mark can evolve subtly — by Book Six the break could close, or open wider, or migrate. That's brand longevity built in.

**Production note:** AI image generators are unreliable for clean vector logos. Recommendation: brief a designer on Fiverr or 99designs with the concept above (budget $80–250), or draw it yourself in Affinity Designer / Figma. The brief is short: *"A Norse interlace knot forming a circular ring, drawn as a single continuous line, with a single small break in the line at the top. Clean vector. Works at 16px and 4K. Reference: 9th-century Urnes-style ornament, simplified."*

### The Wordmark

**The Keepers** in Cormorant Garamond SemiBold, letter-spaced wide (~150 units in Adobe terms / `letter-spacing: 0.15em` in CSS). Lowercase tracking opens the type and gives it gravity.

For the book-one wordmark: **WHAT THE STONES REMEMBER** in Cinzel, smaller, beneath, in Fog color when laid on Stone.

## Trailers — *Concept + Shot Lists*

AI video in 2026 is good enough for assembled-cut trailers. Best workflow: generate each shot as a separate 4–8 second clip in **Sora, Veo 3, Runway Gen-4, or Kling 2.0**, then assemble in CapCut, Premiere, or DaVinci Resolve with sound design and a single sustained low-frequency drone score. Don't try to generate a full trailer in one go — it won't work.

### Trailer 1 — *The 15-Second Teaser* (vertical, for Reels/Shorts/TikTok)

**Total: 15 seconds. 4 shots. No voiceover. One sustained tone underneath, broken once by a sound cue.**

Shot 1 (0:00–0:04) — *Wide static, blue-hour fjord with standing stones in middle distance. Almost no motion. A figure in modern clothing — long coat — visible at small scale walking toward the stones from camera-right. Cold ambient sound.*

> *Wide cinematic shot. A Norse fjord at blue hour, low fog at ground level, three weathered standing stones in middle distance. A single human figure in a modern dark long coat walks toward the stones from the right side of frame. Slow forward dolly. Photorealistic. --ar 9:16 --duration 4s*

Shot 2 (0:04–0:08) — *Hard cut to: close-up of a hotel alarm clock at 3:00 AM, but the numerals on the clock face are Norse runes. Holds. Then begins to glow faintly blue.*

> *Close-up macro shot of a vintage hotel-room digital alarm clock on a nightstand, reading 3:00 AM, but the numerals are Norse runes (Elder Futhark style). The room is dark. The display begins to glow faintly blue-grey over the four-second shot. Cold blue rim light. --ar 9:16 --duration 4s*

Shot 3 (0:08–0:12) — *A woman, sitting upright in bed in a dark room, eyes open in the dark, pendant on her chest catching the same faint blue glow. She mouths two words without sound: "find me."*

> *Medium close-up. A woman in her late thirties with dark hair, sitting upright in a bed in a dark room. Eyes open, looking past camera. A silver pendant on her chest catches faint blue rim light. She mouths two words silently. Cold blue ambient lighting. No sound design in-shot. --ar 9:16 --duration 4s*

Shot 4 (0:12–0:15) — *Hard cut to black. Wordmark fades in over three seconds: **THE KEEPERS / WHAT THE STONES REMEMBER**. The mark beneath. One sound cue — a low stone-on-stone shift — at the moment of cut to black.*

### Trailer 2 — *The 60-Second Trailer* (horizontal, primary)

**Structure: three movements of 20 seconds each.** Hotel-bar register / haunting cracks open / call to action.

**Movement 1 (0:00–0:20) — Modern lives.**

Quick cuts, each 2–3 seconds, all in the modern register, building rhythm.

- Establishing aerial: San Francisco at night, bay bridge lit.
- Marriott elevator opening on a busy hotel floor — laughter spilling out.
- A pint glass clinking against another pint glass. Bokeh of bar lights.
- A group of five at a hotel-bar booth, laughing — Hal in the middle, his expression a half-beat behind the others.
- Conference badge swinging on a lanyard as someone walks fast through a hallway.
- Close-up: Hal at a hotel window, forehead against the glass.

Underneath: low ambient bar sound. A single quiet line of voiceover, female, in the last three seconds — *"He had been saying her name like a question for four years."*

**Movement 2 (0:20–0:40) — The cracks open.**

Cuts slow down. Each shot 4 seconds. The texture inverts from modern to old.

- A man at a four-top in a hotel bar, face slightly out of focus, that won't quite resolve no matter how the shot tries.
- Erin at her Zurich kitchen table, pendant in her hand, eyes red from crying.
- A rune carved into the side of the elevator panel — held for two beats — gone on cut.
- The fjord strand from the prologue, by daylight, empty.
- Kenny in a pub on Lewis, mid-sentence, suddenly silent, hand on his throat.
- Hal at his kitchen table at 3 AM, an envelope open in front of him, hand over his mouth.

Underneath: the ambient bar sound has been replaced by a low stone-resonant drone. One voiceover line: *"There is a thing under the land that should not have woken."*

**Movement 3 (0:40–1:00) — The vow.**

Single sustained wide shot, slow push-in over the full 20 seconds. The standing stones of Callanish in moonlight. Six small human figures walking toward them in procession.

Final 5 seconds: cut to black. Wordmark fades in. Tagline beneath in Cormorant italic: *What we choose to carry, carries us.* Mark beneath that. Domain underneath.

**Voiceover for the final movement, paced across 20 seconds:**

> *"They were not just colleagues."*
> *(beat)*
> *"They were not just friends."*
> *(beat)*
> *"They were the last of a vow none of them remembered making."*
> *(beat)*
> *"And the thing they died once to bind is loose in the world again."*

### Sound Design Notes for Both Trailers

- One sustained low-frequency drone (~40Hz) underneath everything, almost felt rather than heard.
- One stone-on-stone *thunk* used as the hard-cut sound at any major register-shift.
- One vocal element: a single sustained female vowel (an "ah" or an "ohh"), reverb-soaked, used once in each trailer at the moment of greatest tension. Sigrún's voice, in essence.
- No music in the conventional sense. The drone *is* the score.

### Reference Trailers to Watch Before Producing

- *The Northman* (2022) — for the Norse-mood register.
- *Hereditary* (2018) — for the slow-build dread, restraint with sound.
- *Annihilation* (2018) — for the way uncanny visuals reveal themselves slowly.
- *Severance* season opener — for the modern-corporate-but-something-is-wrong feel.

## Tech Identity — *Domain, Org, & Repos*

All of this hangs from **Holdfast Press** as the imprint identity. The domain belongs to the imprint, not the series. The GitHub org belongs to the imprint, not the series. The repos belong inside the org, one per series. Everything compounds.

### Domain — *Locked*

**Primary:** `holdfastpress.com` ✓ *registered*

The `.com` is the safer call for an imprint serving a general-reader audience — universal recognition, no "what's that TLD?" friction, and email at `@holdfastpress.com` reads unambiguously as a publisher's address.

**Defensive registrations to consider** *(optional, $25–60 total):*
- `holdfast.press` — the TLD variant. Worth grabbing if available so no one else can stand up a confusingly similar site. Could redirect to `holdfastpress.com`.
- `holdfastpress.net` and `holdfastpress.co` — standard defensive pattern; cheap insurance.
